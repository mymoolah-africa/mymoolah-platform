Subject MyMoolah Treasury Platform — Cash Withdrawal Policy and request for formal product approval

Dear Colleagues,

We write to share MyMoolah’s formal Cash Withdrawal & Ring-Fencing of Own Funds Policy (POL-020, effective 20 April 2026), and to request Standard Bank’s written product approval, or written no-objection, for the product described below. MyMoolah is a PASA-certified Third-Party Payment Provider and operates the MyMoolah Treasury Platform, which holds customer float in segregated accounts at Standard Bank. The policy operationalises a structural separation between a wallet holder’s own funds (ring-fenced and never withdrawable as cash) and third-party funds received for the wallet holder (the only balance eligible for cash collection through any MyMoolah Cash-Withdrawal Partner — currently eeziCash (Flash Group), EasyPay retail cash-withdrawal, and Cliquefin / OTT cash-withdrawal vouchers, and any future equivalent rail). In our respectful submission this structure places MyMoolah firmly outside the definition of “the business of a bank” in section 1 read with section 11 of the Banks Act 94 of 1990, and is consistent with the SARB Position Paper on Electronic Money (NPS 01/2020) and with the sponsor-bank segregation posture it contemplates.

AProduct structure — Own Funds vs Third-Party Funds

Own Funds

Any amount credited to a MyMoolah wallet from a source where the remitting account holder is, or can reasonably be determined to be, the same natural person as the FICA-verified wallet holder. This includes self-EFT from the holder’s own bank account, self-initiated PayShap credits, self-loaded NFC/Halo deposits, and all voucher-based cash-in.

Third-Party Funds

Any amount credited from a distinct legal or natural person — e.g. an employer paying salary, a corporate client’s disbursement run, a loan disbursement, or a P2P transfer from another wallet holder.

Own Funds are ring-fenced against cash withdrawal. They may be spent freely on wallet-to-wallet transfers, PayShap, EFT, bill payments, airtime, data, electricity and other value-added services, QR merchant payments, NFC spend, and non-cash digital vouchers — but they may not, under any circumstance, be converted to physical cash via any MyMoolah Cash-Withdrawal Partner — eeziCash (Flash Group), EasyPay retail cash-withdrawal, Cliquefin / OTT cash-withdrawal vouchers, USSD cash-withdrawal, or any successor rail. The restriction is absolute; MyMoolah operates no override that would permit Own Funds to be paid out as cash.

Third-Party Funds are eligible for cash redemption, subject to FICA controls, transaction-monitoring rules, and ordinary KYC-tier limits. In that case the cash payment is the delivery of a payment due to the wallet holder from an identified third party — a payment-system activity, not deposit repayment.

BLegal basis — why this is not deposit-taking

For the following four reasons, read cumulatively, MyMoolah’s wallet does not constitute impermissible deposit-taking under the Banks Act:

  1. No repayable amount as against MyMoolah. Own Funds credited to the wallet cannot be withdrawn as cash. MyMoolah is therefore not accepting amounts from the public subject to an obligation to repay a deposit in the sense contemplated by section 1 of the Banks Act. Own Funds may be used (for VAS, transfers, bill payments), but they cannot be repaid as cash.
  2. Cash redemption is payment delivery, not deposit repayment. Third-Party Funds may be paid out as cash because, in substance, MyMoolah is delivering a payment due to the wallet holder from an identified third party. This is a payment-system activity within the scope of PASA’s TPPP framework and the SARB NPS oversight regime under the NPS Act 78 of 1998.
  3. Segregated sponsor-bank holding. Customer float is held via Standard Bank in segregated accounts, consistent with the ring-fenced segregation construct contemplated by the SARB Position Paper on Electronic Money (NPS 01/2020) and by the Banks Act Exemption Notices applicable to non-bank payment schemes. MyMoolah operates no interest-bearing deposit product, no credit extension, and no on-balance-sheet intermediation of customer funds.
  4. System-enforced, auditable separation. The Own Funds – Third-Party Funds distinction is enforced at wallet, ledger, and reconciliation layers and is evidenced by an immutable, hash-chained audit trail, as summarised in Section C below.

CControl environment

Control Mechanism
Wallet-level enforcement Every cash-withdrawal endpoint — eeziCash PIN purchase, EasyPay cash-withdrawal token, Cliquefin / OTT cash-withdrawal voucher, USSD cash-withdrawal, and any future equivalent Cash-Withdrawal Partner — routes through the wallet-level guard Wallet.canCashOut() (an internal code identifier retained for continuity), which asserts balance − restricted_balance ≥ amount before debiting. Failure returns a standardised restriction response to the app; no Cash-Available figure is surfaced to the user.
Ledger-level enforcement Restriction is posted atomically with the inbound deposit as a Journal Entry to the sub-liability account 2100-01-02 “Client Float Liability — Restricted”. Permitted non-cash spend triggers a FIFO release Journal Entry. Every entry is SHA-256 hash-chained.
Deposit classification Inbound deposits are classified at ingress as own_funds, third_party_credit, voucher_deposit, or unknown, using a name-match of the remitter name against the FICA-verified wallet-holder name. Unknown classifications default to restricted (conservative). Every decision is recorded in an append-only audit table.
Reconciliation Hourly automated reconciliation asserts SUM(wallets.restricted_balance) == net credit balance of 2100-01-02. Variance > R0.01 raises an on-call alert and a CCO notification.
Monitoring & reporting Blocked cash-withdrawal attempts are logged and feed the transaction-monitoring rule family under POL-004 (Transaction Monitoring & SAR Policy). CTR monitoring operates against the R24,999.99 FICA threshold; STRs are filed via goAML.
Records Classification, deposit, release, and blocked-attempt records are retained for no less than five years under POL-007 (Data Retention).

DFICA & AML — transparent assessment of PIN-only redemption

We wish to raise proactively a characteristic common to every cash-withdrawal product currently supported — eeziCash (Flash Group), EasyPay retail cash-withdrawal, and Cliquefin / OTT cash-withdrawal vouchers — which we expect Standard Bank’s Compliance function to identify: redemption at the merchant, retailer or trader point occurs on the presentation of a PIN or reference token, without an independent identity verification of the physical cash recipient at the point of dispense. We have assessed this characteristic under our FICA Risk Management and Compliance Programme and consider the residual risk to be acceptable at the individual-transaction level for the following cumulative reasons:

  • the per-credential maximum value configured by each Cash-Withdrawal Partner (e.g. R500 per eeziCash PIN; partner-specific ceilings for EasyPay and Cliquefin / OTT);
  • full FICA verification of the wallet holder at onboarding, who is in every case the originating transactor;
  • a complete end-to-end digital audit trail from wallet debit through voucher generation to partner redemption confirmation;
  • transaction-monitoring rules for unusual voucher-generation patterns, including structuring around the FICA CTR threshold and peer-group deviation; and
  • the ring-fence itself, which ensures that only third-party funds traceable to an identified remitter can fund a cash withdrawal.

We would welcome the opportunity to walk Standard Bank’s Compliance team through this assessment in detail, and we are committed to implementing any additional control measures Standard Bank considers appropriate.

ESARB Draft Directive on Payment Activities

We are actively monitoring the South African Reserve Bank’s Draft Directive on Payment Activities (revised draft published for consultation on 14 November 2025). We recognise that the envisaged activity-based authorisation framework is likely to reclassify cash-withdrawal-at-merchant, money remittance, and scheme / closed-loop payment activities under a new licensing regime, and that both Standard Bank and MyMoolah will need to adapt to that regime in due course. We undertake to engage Standard Bank proactively as the transition timeline becomes clearer, and to ensure continuity of this product within the new framework.
